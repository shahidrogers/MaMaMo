Key Takeaways

The good news

Malaysia is one of the few Asian economies that benefits fiscally from an oil shock. Petronas profits surge to RM67 billion in the peak quarter. PITA revenue alone covers the fuel subsidy bill roughly 5× over. The current account swings to a massive 10.6% of GDP surplus — Malaysia becomes a cash machine. Government debt ratio actually falls from 64% to ~59% of GDP as the fiscal balance flips to surplus. GDP growth slows but doesn't collapse — a 2.5% trough is uncomfortable, not catastrophic.

The bad news

Ordinary Malaysians get crushed. Even with fuel price ceilings held at RM2.05, CPI spikes to 25% through food prices (cooking oil, transport-linked goods), imported inflation via the weaker ringgit, and second-round wage effects. Real wages collapse by ~24% in the peak quarter. Consumption craters. Household debt-to-GDP rises to 89% as incomes shrink while loan stocks remain fixed. The E&E sector (38% of exports) takes a hit from weaker global demand. And if the government eventually has to pass through even partial fuel price increases, inflation gets worse.

The fiscal trap

This scenario exposes Malaysia's fundamental fiscal dilemma. Petronas windfall revenue is temporary and tied to a depleting resource (oil production ~575k bpd, declining). Fuel subsidies are politically permanent — no government has survived removing them. The RM20.6 billion cumulative subsidy bill is manageable only because Petronas covers it many times over. But if the windfall funds new recurring spending (civil service bonuses, expanded cash transfers), the structural deficit reasserts viciously once oil prices normalise. The golden rule: windfall goes to debt reduction and the Kumpulan Wang Amanah, not the operating budget.

What BNM does

BNM faces a classic impossible trinity moment. Inflation screams for rate hikes, but the economy is slowing and households are leveraged at 84%+ of GDP. The model assumes a restrained 50bps hike (OPR 3.0% → 3.5%) — enough to signal credibility without breaking the mortgage market. MGS10 rises to ~4.5% as foreign investors demand a war premium. The ringgit weakens 7-8% on REER but BNM deploys reserves to prevent a rout. If the war extends beyond 4 quarters, reserves (~USD115bn) start looking thinner and a larger depreciation becomes unavoidable.
